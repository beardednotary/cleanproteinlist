Why "Organic" Doesn't Mean Lead-Free: The Certification Gap

Orgain's contamination reveals a critical misunderstanding about organic certification.

What USDA Organic DOES Test

‚úÖ USDA Organic Tests For:

  • Synthetic pesticides and herbicides
  • Genetically modified organisms (GMOs)
  • Synthetic fertilizers
  • Antibiotics (in animal products)
  • Growth hormones
  • Prohibited food additives

‚ùå USDA Organic Does NOT Test For:

  • Lead
  • Cadmium
  • Arsenic
  • Mercury
  • Any naturally-occurring heavy metals
  • Soil contamination levels

The gap is clear: Organic certification prevents synthetic contamination, NOT natural contamination.

Why Organic Products Often Have MORE Lead

Clean Label Project found that organic plant proteins had 3x MORE lead than conventional. Here's why:

1. Increased Soil Interaction

Organic farming encourages beneficial soil microbes and natural nutrient cycling. This ALSO increases plant uptake of heavy metals naturally present in soil. More soil-plant interaction = more metal absorption.

2. Organic Fertilizers Contain Metals

Permitted organic inputs include compost, manure, bone meal, and rock phosphate. These can contain trace heavy metals that accumulate in soil over time.

3. Legacy Contamination Persists

Converting conventional farmland to organic doesn't remove decades of accumulated soil heavy metals. Lead persists in soil for 100+ years.

4. No Heavy Metal Testing Required

USDA Organic audits check farming practices, not end-product heavy metal levels. A product can be "certified organic" with high lead contamination.
