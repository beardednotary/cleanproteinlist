The RTD Shake Testing Gap

Consumer Reports' October 2025 investigation focused primarily on protein powders, with limited testing of ready-to-drink shakes. This left massive gaps in consumer knowledge about the safety of popular RTD products.

Why Consumer Reports Didn't Test Popular RTD Shakes:

Consumer Reports' testing methodology prioritized:

  1. Protein powders over ready-to-drink shakes (15 powders vs 8 RTD shakes tested)
  2. Sports nutrition products marketed to athletes and fitness enthusiasts

This excluded:

Why So Many RTD Shakes Weren't Tested

Consumer Reports had to make choices about which products to test with limited resources. Here's why popular RTD shakes were excluded:

Testing Scope Limitations:

1. Sports Nutrition Focus

Consumer Reports targeted products marketed for athletic performance and muscle building. This excluded:

  • Medical nutrition (Ensure, Boost)
  • Weight loss shakes (SlimFast, Atkins)
  • General wellness shakes (many RTD brands)

2. Product Category Confusion

Some RTD shakes don't fit cleanly into "protein shake" category:

  • Fairlife = "filtered milk" (not marketed as protein shake)
  • Ensure/Boost = "nutritional beverage" (not sports nutrition)
  • Premier Protein = "protein shake" but mass-market category

3. Protein Source Priorities

Consumer Reports prioritized known high-risk categories:

  • Plant proteins (93% failure rate - highest risk)
  • Whey isolate (50% failure rate - moderate risk)
  • Skipped dairy-based whole proteins (perceived lower risk)

4. Budget Constraints

Testing is expensive ($500-1,000 per product):

  • 23 products tested total
  • Only 8 were RTD shakes
  • Hundreds of RTD brands exist in market
